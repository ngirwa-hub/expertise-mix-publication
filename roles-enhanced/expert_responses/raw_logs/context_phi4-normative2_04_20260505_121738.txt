1. **Interoperability Challenges**  
   As systems transition from AC to DC, ensuring compatibility between existing infrastructure and new DC technologies is crucial. Without standardized interfaces and protocols, integrating DC solutions with legacy systems can be complex and costly.

2. **Economic Uncertainty in Return on Investment (ROI)**  
   The initial investment for implementing DC technology can be substantial, leading to uncertainties regarding the timeline and magnitude of ROI. This economic risk may deter stakeholders from committing resources without clear financial incentives or guarantees.

3. **Regulatory and Policy Gaps**  
   Existing regulatory frameworks are primarily designed around AC systems. The absence of comprehensive policies supporting DC adoption can create legal ambiguities, complicating compliance processes and slowing the adoption rate.

4. **Supply Chain Limitations for DC Components**  
   As demand for specialized DC components like converters or cables increases, supply chain constraints could emerge due to limited production capacity or raw material shortages, hindering timely project execution.

5. **Public Perception and Acceptance**  
   Public awareness of DC technology benefits is often low. Misunderstandings about safety, efficiency, and environmental impact can lead to resistance from communities and stakeholders, impacting project approval and implementation.

These barriers highlight the multifaceted challenges in transitioning to DC technologies, requiring strategic planning and coordinated efforts across technical, economic, regulatory, and social dimensions.
