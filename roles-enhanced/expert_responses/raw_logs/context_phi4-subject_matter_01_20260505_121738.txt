1. **Interoperability Challenges**  
   Existing AC infrastructure is deeply entrenched, and integrating DC systems requires overcoming compatibility issues with legacy equipment. This can lead to significant delays and increased costs as new solutions must be developed or existing systems modified.

2. **Economic Uncertainty**  
   The initial investment for transitioning to DC systems can be high due to the need for new components like converters and storage devices. Without clear short-term economic benefits, stakeholders may hesitate to commit financially, especially in markets with fluctuating energy prices.

3. **Regulatory Hurdles**  
   Current electrical codes and standards are predominantly designed for AC systems. Developing and implementing comprehensive regulatory frameworks that accommodate DC technologies involves lengthy processes, which can delay adoption.

4. **Skill Gap in Workforce**  
   The workforce may lack the necessary skills and training to design, install, and maintain DC systems effectively. This gap can result in increased reliance on specialized contractors, driving up costs and slowing project timelines.

5. **Consumer Perception and Acceptance**  
   There is limited awareness and understanding of DC technologies among consumers. Overcoming skepticism and educating end-users about the benefits and safety of DC systems are crucial for widespread acceptance and adoption.
