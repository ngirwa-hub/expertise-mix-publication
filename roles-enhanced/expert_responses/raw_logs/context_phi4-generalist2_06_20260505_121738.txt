1. **Interoperability Challenges**
   - As DC systems become more prevalent, ensuring seamless integration with existing AC infrastructure and various equipment can be complex. The lack of standardized interfaces and communication protocols may hinder the adoption of DC technologies.

2. **Regulatory and Standardization Gaps**
   - Current electrical standards are predominantly designed for AC systems. Insufficient regulatory frameworks and a lack of comprehensive international standards for DC technology could delay implementation, as stakeholders may face uncertainty in compliance requirements.

3. **Skilled Workforce Shortage**
   - The transition to DC requires specialized knowledge that is not widely available among current electricians and engineers trained primarily in AC systems. This skills gap can impede the adoption process due to a shortage of qualified professionals capable of installing, maintaining, and troubleshooting DC systems.

4. **Initial Investment Costs**
   - Despite long-term benefits, the initial capital expenditure required for transitioning to DC infrastructure may deter potential adopters. High upfront costs associated with new equipment, training, and system redesigns can be significant barriers, especially for small-to-medium enterprises.

5. **Public Perception and Awareness**
   - Limited public understanding of the advantages and safety of DC systems might lead to resistance or skepticism among consumers and businesses. Without effective education and awareness campaigns, misconceptions about DC technology could slow its adoption rate.
