1. **Interoperability Challenges**  
   The integration of direct current (DC) technologies with existing alternating current (AC) systems can be complex due to differences in voltage levels, frequency standards, and equipment compatibility. This interoperability issue may require substantial investments in interface solutions or entirely new infrastructure, posing a significant barrier to widespread DC adoption.

2. **Economic Viability Concerns**  
   The cost-effectiveness of transitioning to DC systems remains uncertain for many applications, particularly where existing AC infrastructures are deeply entrenched and economically optimized. The initial capital expenditure for DC installations might not be justified by immediate savings or benefits, especially in regions without strong incentives or subsidies.

3. **Skill Gaps and Workforce Training**  
   There is a notable lack of workforce expertise in designing, installing, and maintaining DC systems. This skills gap can hinder adoption as industries and utilities may face challenges finding qualified personnel to implement and support new DC technologies effectively, leading to delays and increased costs.

4. **Standardization Deficiencies**  
   The absence of universally accepted standards for DC technology components and systems creates uncertainty and risk for potential adopters. Without clear guidelines or industry-wide acceptance criteria, stakeholders might hesitate to invest in DC solutions due to concerns over future compatibility, safety, and regulatory compliance.

5. **Public Perception and Acceptance**  
   General awareness and understanding of the benefits and functionality of DC technologies are limited among consumers and broader public audiences. Misconceptions or a lack of perceived value can lead to resistance against adopting new systems, especially in residential settings where direct benefits may not be immediately apparent.
