## New Barriers to DC Adoption - Shift2DC Expert Elicitation

Here are five new barriers to DC adoption, considering my Senior Renewable Energy Program & Project Manager persona and the Shift2DC project context:

1. **Interoperability Standards Vacuum:** Lack of universally accepted interoperability standards across DC hardware and software vendors will hinder system integration and scalability, leading to vendor lock-in and increased complexity.
2. **Legacy Grid Inertia Impact:** Widespread DC adoption will reduce reliance on AC grid inertia, potentially impacting grid stability and requiring sophisticated control systems to maintain frequency and voltage.
3. **Safety Perception & Training Gaps:** Public and workforce perceptions of DC safety may lag behind reality, requiring significant investment in education and training programs to address concerns and ensure safe operation.
4. **Retrofit Costs & Disruption:** Retrofitting existing AC infrastructure to DC will be expensive and disruptive, making it a less attractive option for many facilities, particularly those with limited capital expenditure budgets.
5. **Supply Chain Vulnerabilities:** Dependence on specialized components (converters, DC cables) could create supply chain bottlenecks and price volatility, especially with geopolitical instability and increasing demand.

1. Lack of Standardized Interoperability
Current DC solutions often lack standardized communication protocols, hindering seamless integration between different vendors' equipment and creating vendor lock-in.

2. Workforce Skill Gap
Existing electrical workforce lacks familiarity and training in DC systems, requiring significant investment in upskilling and potentially slowing down project implementation.

3. Retrofit Costs & Complexity
Integrating DC into existing infrastructure (buildings, ports) presents significant challenges and costs, particularly when dealing with legacy AC equipment and wiring.

4. Limited Availability of DC-Rated Components
The supply chain for DC-rated components (circuit breakers, switches, connectors) is less mature than for AC components, potentially leading to longer lead times and higher costs.

5. Public Safety Perception & Acceptance
Concerns about the safety of DC systems, particularly regarding electric shock hazards, need to be addressed through robust safety measures and public education campaigns.
