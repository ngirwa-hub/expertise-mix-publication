1. **Interoperability Challenges**
   - Existing infrastructure is predominantly AC-based, leading to compatibility issues when integrating DC systems. This requires significant retrofitting or dual-system setups, increasing complexity and costs.

2. **Standardization Gaps**
   - The lack of universally accepted standards for DC voltage levels, connectors, and safety protocols hampers widespread adoption. Without standardization, interoperability between different manufacturers’ equipment becomes challenging.

3. **Market Demand Uncertainty**
   - Limited consumer awareness and understanding of the benefits of DC systems can result in low demand. This uncertainty makes it difficult for businesses to justify investments in new infrastructure or technology development.

4. **Economic Viability Concerns**
   - The initial cost of transitioning from AC to DC, including equipment replacement and system redesign, may outweigh perceived short-term savings. Without clear long-term economic benefits, stakeholders may be reluctant to invest.

5. **Regulatory and Policy Hurdles**
   - Inconsistent regulations across regions can create barriers for the adoption of DC technologies. The lack of supportive policies or incentives makes it challenging for businesses to commit to the transition without financial risk.
