## New Barriers to DC Adoption - Shift2DC Expert Elicitation

Here are five new barriers to DC adoption, considering my Environmental, Regulatory & Socioeconomic Advisor role and the project's scope:

1. **Harmonized International Standards Gap:** Lack of globally harmonized DC voltage and safety standards will create compatibility issues and hinder cross-border project deployment, especially for port applications dealing with varying vessel frequencies.
2. **Legacy Infrastructure Retrofit Costs:** Significant upfront costs associated with retrofitting existing AC infrastructure (buildings, ports, industrial facilities) will be a major deterrent for many stakeholders.
3. **Contractor Skillset Deficiencies:** Current electrical contractors lack the specialized training and experience needed to design, install, and maintain DC systems, leading to increased project risk and potentially lower quality work.
4. **Community Perception & Aesthetic Concerns:** Visual impact of DC infrastructure (cabling, converters) and potential noise from converters could lead to community opposition, requiring proactive engagement and mitigation strategies.
5. **Liability & Insurance Uncertainty:** Existing insurance frameworks and liability models are largely designed for AC systems. Uncertainty surrounding DC system failures and associated liabilities will slow adoption until clarified.

1. **Lack of Standardized Safety Protocols**
Current electrical safety standards are largely built around AC systems. Adapting and creating new safety protocols specifically for DC systems, especially at low and medium voltages, is crucial and currently lagging.

2. **Limited Availability of Skilled Technicians**
Installation, maintenance, and troubleshooting of DC systems require specialized skills not widely available in the current electrical workforce, creating a bottleneck for deployment.

3. **Interoperability Concerns with Legacy Equipment**
Integrating DC systems with existing AC infrastructure and legacy equipment poses significant challenges and may necessitate costly retrofits or replacements.

4. **Supply Chain Vulnerabilities for DC Components**
The supply chain for specialized DC components (cables, converters, etc.) may be less robust than for established AC equipment, potentially leading to delays and price volatility.

5. **Public Perception & “Fear of the Unknown”**
Lack of familiarity with DC technology among the general public can lead to resistance or concerns, hindering acceptance and potentially impacting project permitting.
