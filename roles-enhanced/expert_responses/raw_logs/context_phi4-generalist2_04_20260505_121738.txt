1. **Interoperability Challenges**  
   Transitioning from AC to DC systems involves integrating with existing infrastructure, which is predominantly AC-based. This can lead to compatibility issues across different equipment and technologies, complicating seamless integration.

2. **Initial Cost Barriers**  
   The upfront investment for converting existing systems or building new DC-compatible infrastructure can be significant. This includes costs related to new hardware, training personnel, and updating control systems, which may deter initial adoption despite potential long-term savings.

3. **Lack of Standardization**  
   There is currently a lack of universally accepted standards and protocols for low and medium voltage DC systems. The absence of standardization can hinder widespread adoption as it creates uncertainty regarding compatibility and performance across different applications and regions.

4. **Limited Skilled Workforce**  
   The current workforce may not have the necessary skills or knowledge to install, maintain, and operate DC systems effectively. This gap necessitates extensive training programs, which could delay implementation and increase costs.

5. **Regulatory Uncertainty**  
   Regulatory frameworks for DC technologies are still evolving, leading to uncertainty about compliance requirements. Without clear regulations, organizations may be hesitant to invest in DC solutions due to potential future legal or financial liabilities.
