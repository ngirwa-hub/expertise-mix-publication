1. **Interoperability Challenges**  
   The integration of DC systems with existing AC infrastructure poses significant technical challenges, as compatibility issues can arise. These may lead to inefficiencies or require costly adaptations in legacy systems that are predominantly designed for alternating current.

2. **Standardization Gaps**  
   A lack of universally accepted standards for DC voltage levels and components can hinder widespread adoption. Without standardization, manufacturers and consumers face uncertainty regarding system performance and compatibility, potentially slowing down deployment and innovation.

3. **Skill Shortage in Workforce**  
   The shift to DC technologies necessitates a workforce skilled in new design, installation, and maintenance practices specific to DC systems. A shortage of such trained professionals can delay projects, increase costs, and affect the reliability of DC solutions.

4. **Economic Viability Concerns**  
   Initial investment costs for transitioning to DC infrastructure can be prohibitive, especially for small businesses or regions with limited financial resources. The perceived economic risk may deter stakeholders from adopting DC technologies despite long-term benefits.

5. **Consumer Awareness and Acceptance**  
   Public understanding of the advantages of DC systems is often limited, leading to resistance based on misconceptions or lack of information. Effective education campaigns are needed to build consumer confidence and acceptance, which is crucial for market penetration and adoption success.
