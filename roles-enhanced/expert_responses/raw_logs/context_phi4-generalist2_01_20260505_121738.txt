1. **Cultural Resistance and Knowledge Gap**
   - Many stakeholders, including consumers and businesses, have ingrained habits and familiarity with AC systems. This cultural inertia can hinder the adoption of DC technologies due to perceived risks or lack of understanding about the benefits and functionalities of DC.

2. **Interoperability Challenges**
   - Existing infrastructure is predominantly designed for AC systems, leading to compatibility issues when integrating new DC components. This interoperability challenge requires significant retrofitting efforts and technical solutions to ensure seamless operation between old and new systems.

3. **Economic Incentives and Policy Gaps**
   - The absence of strong economic incentives or supportive policies can deter investment in DC technologies. Without clear financial benefits, such as tax breaks or subsidies, stakeholders may be reluctant to transition from established AC infrastructure to DC systems.

4. **Standardization Shortfalls**
   - A lack of universally accepted standards for DC technology components and applications creates uncertainty and increases costs associated with testing and certification. This can slow down the development and deployment of new DC solutions across different regions and industries.

5. **Supply Chain Constraints**
   - The current supply chain is heavily oriented towards AC technologies, which may lead to shortages or increased costs for DC-specific materials and components like cables and converters. Developing a robust supply chain for DC systems is crucial for widespread adoption but presents significant logistical challenges.
