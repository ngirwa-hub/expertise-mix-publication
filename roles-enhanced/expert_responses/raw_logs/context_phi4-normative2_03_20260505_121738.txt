1. **Market Readiness and Industry Standards**
   - The lack of established industry standards for low and medium voltage DC systems can hinder widespread adoption. Without standardized protocols, interoperability between different manufacturers' equipment becomes challenging, leading to reluctance from industries to adopt DC technologies due to compatibility concerns.

2. **Economic Incentives and Cost Structures**
   - Current economic incentives predominantly favor AC systems, with subsidies and financial models not adequately supporting the transition to DC technologies. The initial investment costs for DC infrastructure can be higher, and without appropriate cost-benefit analyses and financial support mechanisms, stakeholders may find it difficult to justify the switch.

3. **Training and Workforce Development**
   - A significant barrier is the scarcity of skilled professionals trained in DC systems installation, maintenance, and troubleshooting. As the workforce remains predominantly AC-focused, there is a lack of expertise available for the design and implementation of DC solutions, which can slow down adoption rates due to skill shortages.

4. **Public Perception and Awareness**
   - Public understanding and acceptance of DC technologies are limited. Misconceptions about safety, efficiency, or reliability can deter both consumers and businesses from adopting these systems. Enhanced educational campaigns and demonstrative projects are needed to improve awareness and acceptance among end-users.

5. **Regulatory Uncertainty and Policy Gaps**
   - Regulatory frameworks for DC systems are still evolving, leading to uncertainty in compliance requirements. This lack of clear policy guidance can discourage investment as stakeholders face potential legal and regulatory hurdles, making it difficult to plan long-term projects involving DC technologies.
