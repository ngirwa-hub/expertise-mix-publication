1. **Interoperability Challenges**
   - The integration of DC systems with existing AC infrastructure poses significant technical hurdles. Compatibility issues between different manufacturers' equipment, varying standards, and protocols can lead to inefficiencies and increased costs during implementation.

2. **Supply Chain Limitations**
   - There is a limited availability of components such as specialized cables, connectors, and converters required for DC systems. This scarcity can drive up prices and delay project timelines due to longer procurement processes and reliance on global supply chains that may be disrupted.

3. **Skilled Workforce Shortage**
   - The current workforce lacks sufficient training in the design, installation, and maintenance of DC technologies. This gap necessitates extensive education and certification programs, which can slow adoption rates as organizations struggle to find qualified personnel.

4. **Consumer Perception and Acceptance**
   - Public awareness and understanding of the benefits of DC technology are limited. Misconceptions about reliability or efficiency compared to AC systems may lead to resistance from consumers and stakeholders, affecting market penetration and acceptance.

5. **Regulatory Inertia and Uncertainty**
   - Existing regulations are predominantly designed for AC systems, creating a complex environment for the approval and implementation of new DC technologies. The slow pace of regulatory updates can deter investment and innovation in the sector due to uncertainty about compliance requirements.
