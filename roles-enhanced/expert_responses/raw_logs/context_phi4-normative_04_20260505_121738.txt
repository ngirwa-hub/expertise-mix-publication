1. **Interoperability Challenges**
   - Existing infrastructure is predominantly AC-based, creating compatibility issues when integrating DC systems. This can lead to increased costs and technical complexities in adapting current networks to accommodate both AC and DC.

2. **Economic Viability Concerns**
   - High initial investment costs for DC technology deployment may deter adoption, especially if the long-term savings or benefits are not immediately apparent or quantifiable. This includes costs related to new infrastructure, training, and equipment upgrades.

3. **Regulatory Uncertainty**
   - Lack of clear regulatory frameworks specific to DC systems can create uncertainty for stakeholders. Without standardized regulations, companies may be hesitant to invest in technologies that could later become non-compliant or require costly adjustments.

4. **Limited Skilled Workforce**
   - The current workforce lacks expertise in DC technology, leading to a skills gap. This barrier can slow adoption as organizations struggle to find qualified personnel for designing, installing, and maintaining DC systems.

5. **Consumer Awareness and Acceptance**
   - Limited public understanding of the benefits and functionality of DC systems may lead to resistance or lack of interest from consumers. Without adequate awareness campaigns, potential users might not see the value in transitioning to DC solutions.
