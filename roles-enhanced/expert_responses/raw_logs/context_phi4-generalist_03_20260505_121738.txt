1. **Cultural Resistance and Knowledge Gap**
   - Many stakeholders, including consumers and small businesses, may lack awareness or understanding of direct current (DC) benefits compared to traditional alternating current (AC) systems. This knowledge gap can lead to cultural resistance against adopting new technologies, slowing down the transition.

2. **Interoperability Challenges with Existing Infrastructure**
   - Integrating DC technology into existing AC-dominated infrastructure poses significant interoperability challenges. These include compatibility issues between old and new systems, requiring complex retrofitting solutions that could deter adoption due to increased costs or technical difficulties.

3. **Limited Standardization Across Regions**
   - The lack of globally standardized regulations and specifications for DC systems can create barriers to widespread adoption. Different regional standards may necessitate customized solutions, complicating the implementation process and increasing costs.

4. **Economic Viability Concerns in Low-Demand Areas**
   - In regions with lower energy demand or limited access to renewable resources, the economic viability of implementing DC technology might be questionable. The initial investment may not yield sufficient returns compared to AC systems, particularly where existing infrastructure is still functional and cost-effective.

5. **Supply Chain and Manufacturing Constraints**
   - Developing a robust supply chain for DC components, such as cables and converters, can face significant constraints. Limited manufacturing capabilities or reliance on specific materials can lead to increased costs and delays in production, hindering widespread adoption of DC technologies.
