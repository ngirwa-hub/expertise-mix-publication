1. **Interoperability Challenges**
   - The integration of DC systems with existing AC infrastructure poses significant technical challenges. Ensuring seamless interoperability between these two systems requires advanced control strategies, which can complicate system design and increase costs.

2. **Standardization Gaps**
   - Lack of universally accepted standards for low and medium voltage DC technologies hinders widespread adoption. Without standardized components and protocols, it becomes difficult to ensure compatibility and reliability across different applications and regions.

3. **Economic Uncertainty**
   - The initial investment required for transitioning from AC to DC systems can be prohibitive for many organizations. Economic uncertainties, including fluctuating energy prices and uncertain return on investment timelines, may deter adoption despite long-term benefits.

4. **Workforce Training Deficit**
   - There is a significant gap in the current workforce's knowledge and skills related to DC technologies. The need for specialized training programs to equip engineers and technicians with the necessary expertise can delay implementation and increase operational costs.

5. **Regulatory Hurdles**
   - Existing regulations are predominantly designed around AC systems, creating bureaucratic obstacles for DC adoption. Navigating these regulatory frameworks requires time and resources, potentially slowing down project timelines and increasing compliance costs.
