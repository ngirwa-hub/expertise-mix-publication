1. **Integration with Legacy Systems**
   - Many existing infrastructure systems are designed for AC power, and integrating DC can require significant retrofitting or adaptation of current setups. This challenge is exacerbated in environments where legacy equipment cannot be easily replaced due to cost constraints.

2. **Standardization Gaps**
   - Lack of standardized protocols and specifications for DC technologies across regions and industries hinders interoperability and widespread adoption. Standardization efforts are often slow, creating uncertainty for manufacturers and developers looking to invest in these systems.

3. **Perception and Awareness**
   - There is a general lack of awareness and understanding among stakeholders about the benefits and applications of DC power. This can lead to reluctance or hesitation in adopting new technologies due to perceived risks or unknowns associated with shifting from established AC systems.

4. **Economic Feasibility Concerns**
   - Initial investment costs for transitioning to DC systems, including infrastructure modifications and technology acquisition, are often seen as prohibitive by potential adopters. This economic barrier can deter both private sector investments and public funding support.

5. **Regulatory Uncertainty**
   - Regulatory frameworks around the adoption of DC technologies are still evolving. The uncertainty in regulations can discourage stakeholders from committing to DC projects due to potential future compliance issues or changes in policy that might affect project viability.
