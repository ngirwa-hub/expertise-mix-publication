1. **Interoperability Challenges**
   - Direct current (DC) systems often lack compatibility with existing AC infrastructure, leading to significant integration challenges during retrofitting or new installations. This can result in increased costs and technical complexities.

2. **Standardization Deficit**
   - The absence of universally accepted standards for DC technologies hampers their adoption as manufacturers and users face uncertainty regarding specifications and performance benchmarks. This lack of standardization complicates the development, approval, and implementation processes.

3. **Economic Uncertainty**
   - Initial costs associated with transitioning to or implementing DC systems can be high due to new equipment requirements and potential system redesigns. Without clear economic incentives or cost-benefit analyses demonstrating long-term savings, stakeholders may hesitate to invest in DC technology.

4. **Skill Gap in Workforce**
   - The current workforce may lack the necessary skills and training to design, install, maintain, and operate DC systems effectively. This skill gap can lead to delays, increased costs for specialized labor, and potential operational inefficiencies during deployment.

5. **Perception and Awareness Issues**
   - There is often limited awareness or understanding of the benefits and applications of DC technology among decision-makers and end-users. Misconceptions about efficiency, reliability, and safety may hinder acceptance and willingness to transition from established AC systems.
