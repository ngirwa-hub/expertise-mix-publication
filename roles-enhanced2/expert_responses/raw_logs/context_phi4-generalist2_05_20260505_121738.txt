1. **Interoperability Challenges**
   - As different regions, industries, and devices may have varying standards for DC systems, ensuring seamless integration across these platforms can be challenging. This lack of standardization could hinder widespread adoption due to compatibility issues.

2. **Economic Viability in Low-Demand Areas**
   - In areas with low electricity demand or where existing AC infrastructure is still relatively new, the cost-benefit ratio may not justify transitioning to DC systems. Without significant economic incentives, stakeholders might be reluctant to invest in DC technologies.

3. **Technological Maturity and Reliability Concerns**
   - Many DC solutions are still emerging technologies, and there may be concerns about their long-term reliability and performance compared to established AC systems. Until these technologies prove themselves over time, hesitation to adopt them could persist.

4. **Supply Chain Limitations**
   - The current supply chain for DC components, such as converters or specialized cables, might not be robust enough to meet large-scale demands quickly. This limitation can delay projects and increase costs, acting as a barrier to adoption.

5. **Regulatory Hurdles and Uncertainty**
   - Inconsistent regulations across different jurisdictions regarding DC systems could create uncertainty for businesses and investors. Navigating these regulatory landscapes may require additional resources and time, slowing down the deployment of DC technologies.
