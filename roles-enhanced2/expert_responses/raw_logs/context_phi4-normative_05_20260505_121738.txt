1. **Interoperability Challenges**  
   The integration of direct current (DC) systems with existing alternating current (AC) infrastructure can lead to significant compatibility issues. This includes challenges in interfacing DC technologies with legacy AC equipment, which may require costly upgrades or specialized adapters to ensure seamless operation.

2. **Cost and Economic Feasibility**  
   Initial investment costs for implementing DC systems are often higher than those for AC systems due to the need for new infrastructure, conversion devices, and training. These upfront expenses can deter adoption, particularly in sectors where cost-efficiency is a primary concern.

3. **Regulatory and Standardization Hurdles**  
   The lack of standardized regulations and guidelines for DC technologies creates uncertainty for stakeholders. This regulatory ambiguity can slow down the development and deployment of DC solutions as companies await clear frameworks to ensure compliance and safety.

4. **Technical Knowledge Gap**  
   There is a shortage of skilled professionals with expertise in DC technology design, installation, and maintenance. This knowledge gap hinders effective implementation and support, leading to potential operational inefficiencies and increased reliance on external contractors.

5. **Consumer Acceptance and Awareness**  
   Limited public awareness and understanding of the benefits of DC systems can result in resistance to adoption. Overcoming misconceptions and educating consumers about the advantages of DC technologies is crucial for broader acceptance and integration into everyday applications.
