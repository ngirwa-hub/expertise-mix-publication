1. **Interoperability Challenges**  
   Existing electrical infrastructure is predominantly AC-based, leading to compatibility issues when integrating DC systems. This requires significant modifications or hybrid solutions, increasing complexity and cost.

2. **Standardization Deficiencies**  
   Lack of universally accepted standards for low and medium voltage DC technologies hampers widespread adoption. Varying regional standards can lead to confusion and hinder interoperability across different jurisdictions.

3. **Skilled Workforce Shortage**  
   The current workforce is largely trained in AC systems, resulting in a skills gap for designing, installing, and maintaining DC infrastructure. This necessitates extensive training programs, which can delay implementation and increase costs.

4. **Economic Viability Concerns**  
   Initial investment costs for transitioning to or incorporating DC technologies are often high compared to existing AC solutions. The economic benefits of DC adoption may not be immediately apparent, discouraging stakeholders from investing without clear long-term financial incentives.

5. **Regulatory Uncertainty**  
   Regulatory frameworks and policies for DC systems are still evolving, leading to uncertainty about compliance requirements and potential future regulations. This can deter investment due to the risk of unforeseen regulatory changes affecting project viability.
