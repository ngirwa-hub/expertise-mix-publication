1. **Interoperability Challenges**  
   The lack of standardized protocols for communication between different DC components can lead to compatibility issues, complicating integration efforts across various systems and devices.

2. **Economic Incentives Misalignment**  
   Current financial incentives may favor AC technologies due to established infrastructures and economies of scale, making it economically challenging to justify the initial investment in DC solutions without policy adjustments.

3. **Skill Gap in Workforce**  
   A shortage of skilled professionals trained specifically in DC technology can hinder adoption, as existing electrical engineers and technicians are predominantly educated in AC systems.

4. **Supply Chain Limitations**  
   The nascent nature of DC markets means that the supply chain for components like cables and converters is underdeveloped, leading to higher costs and longer lead times compared to more established AC technologies.

5. **Cultural Resistance to Change**  
   Organizational inertia and resistance from stakeholders accustomed to AC systems can slow down the transition, as there may be reluctance to adopt new technologies perceived as risky or unnecessary.
